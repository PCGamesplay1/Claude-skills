## Core Principle

Cinema is a sophisticated **emotional delivery system** that bypasses cognitive processing to elicit visceral responses through a multimodal network of visual, auditory, and narrative signals.

---

## I. Biological & Psychological Foundations

### Mirror Neuron System

- Neurons activate both when we perform an action AND when we observe someone else doing it
- Creates **"emotional contagion"** — emotions spread between individuals like a viral infection
- Mediated by the **insula** (bridge between mirror neurons and emotional centres)
- Group viewing amplifies emotional intensity — neural synchronisation occurs across the audience
- Allows movement from simple contagion → true empathy (understanding character feelings while retaining own identity)

### Affective Disposition Theory (ADT)

- Emotional engagement depends on viewer's **moral evaluation of characters**
- Positive affect when liked characters succeed / bad things happen to disliked ones
- **"Justice consideration"**: if disliked character punished too severely → viewers become disturbed rather than satisfied
- **Make the protagonist morally legible before the comedy or danger hits**

### Uncanny Valley

- Digital characters lacking subtle human nuances trigger **cognitive dissonance** rather than empathy
- Triggers: asymmetric expressions, unrealistic blink rates, mechanical movement
- Modern AI video is closing this gap via micro-expression reproduction and emotional flow timing

## II. Visual Cues & Grammar

### Luminant Dramaturgy (Lighting as Emotional Heavy-Lifter)

| Lighting Style | Emotional Output |
|---|---|
| **High-Key** (even, minimal shadow) | Happiness, joy, humour |
| **Low-Key** (high contrast, deep shadow) | Mystery, suspense, malice, intrigue |
| **Available Light** | Realism, grittiness, authenticity |
| **Underlight** | Negative emotions, distortion of features |
| **Toplight** | Hides eyes in shadow — restricts cognitive engagement with character's inner state |
| **Silhouette** | Unknown, threatening — completely hides facial features |

### Chromatic Drivers (Colour as Subconscious Language)

| Colour | Psychological Response |
|---|---|
| Red | Passion, danger, power, urgency |
| Blue | Calm, sadness, trust, isolation |
| Yellow | Happiness, anxiety, caution |
| Green | Growth, envy, tranquility |
| Purple | Mystery, royalty, spirituality |
| Black | Sophistication, death, secrecy |
| White | Purity, emptiness, hope |

**Emotional Tree Branching**: unfamiliar colour-coded spaces provoke exceptionally strong outbursts of imagination — animators exploit this by leading audiences into imaginary spaces where colour provides primary emotional context.

### Animation Principles

- **Squash & Stretch**: depicts form flattened/extended to emphasise impact; communicates weight, energy, emotion
- **Exaggeration**: makes character intent "clear, almost obvious" — amplifies what would otherwise be missed
- Character design: larger eyes = youth/innocence (baby schema) → evokes empathy; broad shoulders = raw power; angular lines = menace

## III. Auditory Architecture

### Sound: The Secret Weapon

- Brain processes auditory stimuli **faster than visual** — triggers immediate emotional reactions
- Sharp, high-pitched sound → alarm; soft melodies → peace
- In animation: every footstep/rustle created from scratch — Foley is even more essential than live-action

### Emotional Prosody (Voice Performance)

| Emotion | Pitch | Tempo | Loudness | Quality |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hot Anger | High, limited fluctuation | Fast/Irregular | Loud | Bright, sharp |
| Sadness | Narrow, low | Slow | Quiet | Thin, "broken" |
| Joy/Elation | Wide, high | Fast | Loud | Bright, resonant |
| Anxiety | Middle | Irregular | Quiet | Tense, jittery |
| Boredom | Low, flat | Slow | Quiet | Flat, monotonous |

Voice actors use "vocal stereotypes" to quickly establish character. Fast tempo = excitement/urgency; slow tempo = contemplation/sadness.

### Diegetic vs Non-Diegetic

- **Diegetic** (heard by characters): grounds audience in reality, fosters immersion
- **Non-diegetic** (score only): invisible emotional guide
- **Anempathetic sound**: pairing tragic scene with "indifferent" happy music → creates cognitive tension and irony; forces deeper reflection

## IV. Narrative & Editing Frameworks

### Eisenstein's Montage Theory (5 Types)

| Type | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Metric** | Edit timing creates rhythmic effect |
| **Rhythmic** | Movement within shots guides pace |
| **Tonal** | Sequential tonal/colour consistency conveys mood |
| **Overtonal** | Combines metric + rhythmic + tonal for visceral response |
| **Intellectual** | Contrasting unrelated images trigger abstract ideas (funeral + happy flashback = bittersweet nostalgia) |

### Walter Murch's Rule of Six (Editing Priorities)

| Priority | Weight |
|---|---|
| **Emotion** | 51% |
| Story | 23% |
| Rhythm | 10% |
| Eye-trace | 7% |
| 2D plane of screen | 5% |
| 3D space (continuity) | 4% |

> Cut on emotion first. If the timing feels right emotionally, viewers won't notice continuity issues.

### Focalization (Perspectival Filter)

- Audience knows more than character → sympathy/dread
- Character knows more than audience → mystery/intrigue
- Audience and character know the same → direct empathy

### Narrative Structures for Empathy

- **Freytag's Pyramid**: Exposition → Rising Action → Climax → Falling Action → Catastrophe
- **Save the Cat Beat Sheet**: 15-beat structure (Blake Snyder); keeps emotional fluctuation active across 90–150 min runtimes

## V. Mood-Cue Workflow (Actionable)

### Step 1 — Establish Dominant Mood

Use **redundant emotive cues** (lighting + music + set design together) early in the scene. Multiple redundant cues increase probability that different viewers all receive the signal.

### Step 2 — Deploy Emotion Markers

At critical story beats, use **highly coordinated clusters of cues** (musical stinger + close-up + practical lighting change). "Refreshes" audience's emotional state and prevents mood erosion.

### Step 3 — Cut on Emotion (Murch)

When editing, prioritise emotional truth over dialogue timing or visual continuity.

### Step 4 — Manage Emotional Inertia

Emotions have bodily inertia — the body cannot change arousal states as quickly as the mind changes thoughts. Use **transitional sequences** (a storm, a musical bridge, a slow tracking shot) to give viewers time to shift emotional orientation between radical states.

### Step 5 — Control Information Flow

- Audience knows more than character → sympathy/dread
- Character knows more than audience → mystery/intrigue
- Audience and character know the same → direct empathy

## VII. Case Study: Pixar's Up — "Married Life" Sequence

- **Visual**: No dialogue — wordless emotional journey through compressed life events
- **Cues**: Nostalgia as primary trigger (Pete Docter's "Polaroid home movies" concept)
- **ADT**: Audience is morally aligned with Carl immediately — making his grief devastating
- **Emotional Inertia**: Rapid montage designed to overwhelm — no time to recover before loss hits
- **Non-diegetic**: Michael Giacchino's score carries all emotional signalling
